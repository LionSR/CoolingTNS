\section{Comparison with the Variational Quantum Algorithm}

To provide a finite-size variational baseline for the cooling simulations, we solve the same non-integrable Ising model using a variational quantum algorithm (VQA) with brickwork circuits of varying depths. The VQA is a hybrid quantum-classical approach that uses a parameterized quantum circuit to prepare trial states and a classical optimizer to minimize the energy expectation value. The comparison should be read as an exploratory diagnostic at the stated system size, circuit depth, noise model, and tensor-network truncation, not as a general statement about the relative scalability or noise robustness of the two approaches.

The VQA simulations are performed using the PastaQ.jl library, which provides a framework for designing and simulating quantum circuits. The gradients required for the classical optimization are obtained by autodifferentiating the noisy matrix product operators (MPO) that represent the quantum circuits.

We employ a hardware-efficient variational ansatz~\citep{Kandala2017Hardwareefficient}, which consists of alternating layers of single-qubit rotations and entangling gates, as depicted in Fig.~\ref{fig:QVE_ansatz}. The single-qubit rotations are parametrized by Euler angles $\mathbf{\theta}$ and implemented as a sequence of $R_z(\theta_1^{q,i})$, $R_x(\theta_2^{q,i})$, and $R_z(\theta_3^{q,i})$ gates, where $q$ identifies the qubit and $i$ refers to the depth position. The entangling gates $U_{\text{ENT}}$ are fixed two-qubit operations, such as controlled-NOT (CNOT) gates, that introduce entanglement between neighboring qubits.

The trial states are prepared by applying the variational circuit to the initial state $\ket{+}^{\otimes N}$, where $N$ is the number of qubits. The depth of the circuit, denoted by $d$, determines the expressiveness of the ansatz. The total number of variational parameters in the circuit is $p=N(3d+1)$.

To estimate the energy expectation value of the prepared trial states, the VQA measures the individual Pauli terms in the Hamiltonian and combines the results. These measurements are subject to statistical noise due to the finite number of samples. The energy estimates are then used to update the variational parameters using a gradient-based optimizer, such as the Adam algorithm.

In Fig.~\ref{fig:VQEoptimization}, we present the results of the VQA optimization for a system size of $N=10$ qubits and a circuit depth of $d=1$, corresponding to $p=40$ variational parameters. We compare the performance of the VQA in the presence and absence of noise, with a noise strength of $p_e=0.01$ for the noisy case. The energy density and ground state overlap are plotted as a function of the number of optimization steps.

In the plotted $N=10$, $d=1$ example, the noiseless VQA optimization lowers the energy and increases the reported ground-state overlap during the optimization window. The noisy run has a slower decrease of the plotted energy and a higher final energy density than the noiseless run for the same ansatz and optimizer. This finite-size example indicates sensitivity to the chosen noise model, but it does not by itself establish the large-system performance of VQA or the optimality of the chosen ansatz.

The cooling data in this note should be interpreted with the same care. In the historical fixed-bond simulations, the implemented cooling channel can lower the late-time energy in some parameter regimes and under some noisy trajectories. These observations do not constitute a controlled robustness theorem or a high-fidelity preparation result. A quantitative comparison with VQA requires the same ingredients used elsewhere in this note: exact-diagonalization checks at small $N$, bond-dimension convergence, trajectory or sampling convergence, and a clearly specified noise model.

Thus the comparison is best viewed as a diagnostic of two different mechanisms for reducing the energy in finite simulations: variational optimization within a chosen circuit family, and repeated system-bath evolution within a chosen truncation and reset protocol. Which mechanism is preferable is a numerical and physical question to be decided only after the corresponding convergence checks have been performed.
