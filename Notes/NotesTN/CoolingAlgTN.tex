\section{Cooling Algorithm}

Consider a spin system consisting of $N$ particles on a lattice governed by a local Hamiltonian $H_S$. We describe a cooling algorithm that allows for the preparation of the ground state of the Hamiltonian~\cite{Terhal2000Problem,Kraus2008Preparation,Diehl2008Quantum,Verstraete2009Quantum,Shabani2016Artificial,Metcalf2020Engineered,Polla2021Quantum,Shtanko2021Algorithms}.
In nature, the cooling of hot objects occurs when they are coupled to cooler reservoirs. The cooling algorithm mimics this process. It consists of multiple cycles, and in each cycle, the energy of the system is reduced via its interaction with a fresh ancillary bath $B$, which consists of $N_B$ particles initialized at the ground state of its Hamiltonian $H_B$. To ensure scalability, the number of ancillas $N_B$ is taken to be extensive, i.e., proportional to the system size $N$. This is necessary to handle the extensive entropy increase due to noise, which a single ancilla would not be sufficient to counteract.
The Hamiltonian of the composite system is given by
\begin{equation}
	H = H_S \otimes \Id_B + \Id_S \otimes H_B + gV,
	\label{eq:Ham}
\end{equation}
where $H_S$ and $H_B$ are the Hamiltonians of the system and the bath, respectively, $\Id_S$ and $\Id_B$ are the identity operators on the system and the bath, respectively, $V$ is the interaction between the system and the bath, and the unit-free small parameter $g$ controls the strength of the interaction.
Without loss of generality, we assume $V = \sum_k V_S^k \otimes V_B^k$ with $V_S^k$ acting on the system and $V_B^k$ acting on the bath.
For notational convenience, we will also use $H_S + H_B$ to denote $H_S \otimes \Id_B + \Id_S \otimes H_B$, and similarly for $H_S + H_B + gV$, etc.

Let us denote the initial state of the system $S$ at the beginning of the $k$-th cycle by $\rho_k$. At the beginning of each cycle, we always bring in a fresh ancilla $B$, initialized at the ground state of its Hamiltonian $H_B$, denoted by $\ket{0}$. The initial density matrix $\rho(0) = \rho_k \otimes \ket{0}\bra{0}$ of the total system evolves according to the Schr\"{o}dinger equation
\begin{equation}
	\rho(t) = e^{-i(H_S + H_{B} + gV) t} \left(\rho_k \otimes \ket{0}\bra{0}\right)  e^{i(H_S + H_{B} + gV) t}.
	\label{eq:rhot}
\end{equation}
After a joint evolution of time $t$, the bath $B$ is discarded and the state of the system $S$ becomes
\begin{equation}
	\rho_{k+1} = \tr_B\left\{ e^{-i(H_S + H_{B} + gV) t} \left(\rho_k \otimes \ket{0}\bra{0}\right)  e^{i(H_S + H_{B} + gV)t} \right\}.
	\label{eq:rhokp1}
\end{equation}
where $\tr_B\{\cdot\}$ denotes the partial trace over the bath degrees of freedom.
An evolution under the Hamiltonian $H$ conserves the energy corresponding to the same Hamiltonian $\langle H_S + H_B + gV\rangle$.
In the case of weak interaction $\min\{\|H_B\|,\|H_S\|\} \gg g > 0$, the expectation value $\langle H_S + H_B\rangle$ is almost conserved. As the ancillary system $B$ is excited from its ground state to excited states (an increase of $\langle H_B \rangle$), the energy exchange between the system $S$ and the bath $B$ leads to a decrease of $\langle H_S \rangle$, indicating that the system is cooled.

To study the performance of the cooling algorithm and optimize its parameters, we employ efficient numerical simulation methods based on tensor networks. Specifically, we use matrix product operator (MPO) and matrix product state (MPS) techniques to simulate the cooling dynamics for large system sizes, exploiting the low-entanglement structure of the steady states. These methods will be discussed in detail in the following sections.

\section{Numerical Simulation Methods}
\label{sec:NumMethods}

In this section, we present efficient numerical methods to simulate the cooling algorithm for large system sizes, focusing on matrix product operator (MPO) and matrix product state (MPS) quantum trajectory simulations. These tensor network techniques enable us to study the performance of the algorithm and optimize its parameters by exploiting the low-entanglement structure of the steady states. We also simulate the effects of noise to investigate the robustness of the cooling algorithm in realistic scenarios.

For a more detailed explanation of the procedure, let us first review the definition of matrix product state and the matrix product operator. A matrix product state (MPS) of a chain of $N$ spins is described as (also see Fig.~\ref{fig:MPS} for an illustration)
\begin{equation}
	\ket{\psi_{\text{MPS}}} = \sum_{i_1,i_2,\ldots,i_N} \tr (A^{[1]}_{i_1}A^{[2]}_{i_2} \ldots A^{[N]}_{i_N})\ket{i_1i_2 \ldots i_N},
\end{equation}
where $i_k = 1,\ldots,d$ and $A^{[k]}_{i_k}$s are $D \times D$ matrices on site $k$ with $D$ being the bond dimension of the MPS, except for the first and the last, which are $1 \times D$ and $D \times 1$ vectors. The total number of parameters involved is $ND^2d$ as compared to $d^N$ in the generic case.

The class of matrix product density operators has a form similar to MPS, except now each tensor $A^{[s]}$ has two physical indices, one for the ket and one for the bra layer. A tensor network diagram illustration is given in Fig.~\ref{fig:MPO}. Mathematically,
\begin{equation}
	\rho_{\text{MPO}} = \sum A_{i_{1} j_{1}}^{[1]} A_{i_2 j_2}^{[2]} \cdots A_{i_{N} j_N}^{[N]}\ket{i_{1}, \ldots, i_{N}}\bra{j_{1}, \ldots, j_{N}},
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the length of the chain, the sum runs over $i_j,j_k = 1,\ldots,d$ with $d$ being the local Hilbert space dimension, and the $A_{i_k j_k}^{[k]}$ are $D \times D$ matrices, except $A_{i_1 j_1}^{[1]}$ and $A_{i_N j_N}^{[N]}$ are $1 \times D$ and $D \times 1$ matrices respectively.  For both states the normalization of the wavefunction is omitted.

\paragraph{Couplings}
In our numerical simulations, we consider cooling with an extensive number of ancillas, i.e., $N_B = N_S$. The couplings between the system and the bath are taken to be $\sigma_X \otimes \sigma_X$ interactions, locally connected from ancillas to the system particles, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:SysBath}.

\subsection{Mode Diagnostics for the Integrable Ising Benchmarks}
\label{sec:TNModeDiagnostics}

For the transverse-field Ising benchmarks, the tensor-network diagnostics use the
same Jordan--Wigner convention and fermionic boundary-condition convention as
the exact-diagonalization diagnostics in \texttt{NotesED/MapToSpin.tex}.  The
Hamiltonian implemented in the code is
\begin{equation}
	H_{\mathrm{code}}=J\sum_j\sigma^z_j\sigma^z_{j+1}+h\sum_j\sigma^x_j.
\end{equation}
It is related to the notes-basis Hamiltonian
\begin{equation}
	H_{\mathrm{notes}}=\frac{\cos\theta}{2}\sum_j\sigma^x_j\sigma^x_{j+1}
	+\frac{\sin\theta}{2}\sum_j\sigma^z_j
\end{equation}
by a global $R_y(\pi/2)^{\otimes N}$ rotation and the scale
\begin{align}
	\theta&=\operatorname{atan}(h,J),&
	\Lambda&=2\sqrt{J^2+h^2},\\
	U&=R_y(\pi/2)^{\otimes N},&
	H_{\mathrm{code}}
	&=\Lambda\,U H_{\mathrm{notes}}U^\dagger .
\end{align}
Consequently the conserved parity used to choose the Fourier grid is
$P_x=\prod_j\sigma^x_j$ in the code basis.  If the spin boundary condition is
$g_I=+1$ for periodic boundary conditions and $g_I=-1$ for anti-periodic
boundary conditions, then
\begin{equation}
	g_F=-g_I P_x.
\end{equation}
Thus, for spin PBC, the $P_x=+1$ sector uses fermionic APBC and half-integer
momenta, while the $P_x=-1$ sector uses fermionic PBC and integer momenta; for
spin APBC these assignments are interchanged.

Within a fixed sector, the derivation uses the full allowed fermionic grid
$\phi_k=2\pi k/N$ and the notes Fourier convention
\begin{equation}
	\tilde a_k=\frac{1}{\sqrt N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}e^{-in\phi_k}a_n .
\end{equation}
The Bogoliubov block for a generic pair $(k,-k)$ is
\begin{equation}
	\tilde H_k=
	\begin{pmatrix}
		w_k & i r_k\\
		-i r_k & -w_k
	\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Here
\begin{equation}
	w_k=\sin\theta-\cos\theta\cos\phi_k,\qquad
	r_k=-\cos\theta\sin\phi_k.
\end{equation}
We use the same Bogoliubov-angle convention as in
\texttt{NotesED/MapToSpin.tex}: for generic modes,
\begin{align}
	\tan(2\varphi_k)&=\frac{r_k}{w_k},\label{eq:tn_bogoliubov_angle}\\
	\hat a_k&=\cos(\varphi_k)\tilde a_k
	+i\sin(\varphi_k)\tilde a_{-k}^\dagger .
	\label{eq:tn_bogoliubov_transform}
\end{align}
For the special modes with $\sin\phi_k=0$, there is no Bogoliubov mixing and
we set $\varphi_k=0$.
The ED and tensor-network implementations evaluate the same diagnostics after
the relabeling $k\mapsto -k$:
\begin{align}
	\tilde a^{\mathrm{code}}_k
	=\tilde a^{\mathrm{notes}}_{-k}
	&=\frac{1}{\sqrt N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}e^{+in\phi_k}a_n,\\
	r^{\mathrm{code}}_k&=+\cos\theta\sin\phi_k.
\end{align}
Equivalently, $r^{\mathrm{code}}_k=-r_k$ and
$\varphi_k^{\mathrm{code}}=-\varphi_k$ after this relabeling.  The mode
energy, coefficient, and occupation formulas below are invariant under this
relabeling.  With either convention,
\begin{equation}
	\epsilon_k=\sqrt{w_k^2+r_k^2}
	=\sqrt{1-\sin(2\theta)\cos\phi_k}.
\end{equation}
The code-unit quasiparticle energy is $\Lambda\epsilon_k$.  The ED, MPS, and MPO
routines store the individual Bogoliubov mode observable
\begin{equation}
	h_k=2\hat a_k^\dagger\hat a_k-1,\qquad
	n_k^{\mathrm{Bog}}=\frac{1+\langle h_k\rangle}{2}.
\end{equation}
This $n_k^{\mathrm{Bog}}$ is the quasiparticle occupation; it is not the raw
momentum occupation $\langle\tilde a_k^\dagger\tilde a_k\rangle$, which equals
$\sin^2\varphi_k$ in the Bogoliubov vacuum for generic modes.  The exact energy
reconstruction used to compare ED and tensor-network results is
\begin{align}
	\langle H_{\mathrm{code}}\rangle
	&=\frac{\Lambda}{2}\sum_{k\in\mathrm{grid}(g_F)}
	\operatorname{coeff}_k\,\langle h_k\rangle ,
	\label{eq:TNModeEnergyReconstruction}\\
	\operatorname{coeff}_k=
	\begin{cases}
		\epsilon_k, & \sin\phi_k\neq 0,\\
		w_k, & \sin\phi_k=0.
	\end{cases}
\end{align}
The signed special-mode coefficient $w_k$ is essential.  Replacing it by
$|w_k|$, or writing a mode contribution as $\epsilon_k n_k$ without specifying
which occupation is meant, gives a different quantity.

The same distinction enters the bath-detuning convention.  The HDF5 dataset
\texttt{mode\_ek\_values} stores the positive single-quasiparticle energies
$\Lambda\epsilon_k$ used to label the measured modes.  For the default
\texttt{XX} cooling channel in the code basis, however, the system-side
operator is $\sigma^x_j$, which commutes with
$P_x=\prod_j\sigma^x_j$.  The dynamics is therefore parity-preserving in the
integrable Ising system.  The lowest generic excitation accessible from a fixed
parity sector has energy
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:mode-pair-threshold}
	\Delta_{\mathrm{pair}}
	=2\Lambda \min_{\sin\phi_k\neq 0}\epsilon_k ,
\end{equation}
not $\Lambda\min_k\epsilon_k$.  The large-\(N\) mode-resolved driver uses this
two-quasiparticle scale as its automatic analytic detuning reference when no
explicit detuning interval is supplied.

\subsection{Matrix Product Operator Simulation}
\label{sec:MPOCooling}

To simulate the MPO cooling dynamics, the implementation first stores the
combined system and bath in the interleaved order
\begin{equation}
	(s_1,b_1,s_2,b_2,\ldots,s_N,b_N).
\end{equation}
This ordering is important: the local system-bath interaction acts on adjacent
sites $(s_i,b_i)$, while the nearest-neighbor Ising interaction between
$s_i$ and $s_{i+1}$ becomes a local three-site operator on
$(s_i,b_i,s_{i+1})$, with the bath site acted on by the identity.

For one Trotter slice of duration $\delta t$, the code applies a symmetric
second-order sweep.  In the notation of Eq.~\eqref{eq:HIsing}, let
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		h_i^{\mathrm{loc}}
		&= h_x \sigma^x_{s_i}+h_z \sigma^z_{s_i}
		+\frac{\Delta}{2} \sigma^{\beta}_{b_i}
		+g\sum_a \sigma^{\mu_a}_{s_i}\sigma^{\nu_a}_{b_i},\\
		h_i^{ZZ}
		&=J\sigma^z_{s_i}\Id_{b_i}\sigma^z_{s_{i+1}}.
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Here $\sigma^\beta_{b_i}$ is the bath Pauli operator selected by the coupling
channel, and the sum over $a$ is the corresponding local system-bath coupling.
For the transverse-field Ising model used in the integrable tests, the
$h_z\sigma^z_{s_i}$ term is absent and $h_x$ is replaced by the transverse
field parameter $h$.

The gates are applied in the following order:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		& e^{-i h_1^{\mathrm{loc}}\delta t/2},\ldots,
		e^{-i h_N^{\mathrm{loc}}\delta t/2},\\
		& e^{-i h_1^{ZZ}\delta t/2},\ldots,
		e^{-i h_{N-1}^{ZZ}\delta t/2},\\
		& e^{-i h_{N-1}^{ZZ}\delta t/2},\ldots,
		e^{-i h_1^{ZZ}\delta t/2},\\
		& e^{-i h_N^{\mathrm{loc}}\delta t/2},\ldots,
		e^{-i h_1^{\mathrm{loc}}\delta t/2}.
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Thus the tensor-network Trotter path is not the standard even--odd split of
the system Hamiltonian alone.  It is an interleaved two- and three-site gate
decomposition of the full system-bath Hamiltonian.  The requested cycle time
$t$ is divided into $M$ slices with $\delta t=t/M$; smaller $\delta t$ reduces
the Trotter error but increases the number of gate applications.  After every
gate application, singular values are truncated to control the MPO bond
dimension.

The mathematical density-matrix channel for one Trotter slice is
\begin{equation}
	\rho_{SB}\longmapsto U_{\delta t}\rho_{SB}U_{\delta t}^\dagger,
	\qquad
	U_{\delta t}=G_mG_{m-1}\cdots G_1,
	\label{eq:trotter_density_slice}
\end{equation}
where the $G_j$ are the two- and three-site gates in the order displayed
above.  Thus one noiseless cooling cycle is
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal C(\rho_S)=
	\tr_B\!\left[
	U_t\left(\rho_S\otimes\ket{\Omega_B}\bra{\Omega_B}\right)U_t^\dagger
	\right],
	\qquad U_t=(U_{\delta t})^M .
	\label{eq:tn_density_cycle}
\end{equation}
Here \(\ket{\Omega_B}\) is the coupling-dependent ground state of the bath
Hamiltonian, for example the negative-eigenvalue state of the selected bath
Pauli operator.
This is the same channel convention used by the exact-diagonalization
implementation.  In the ITensor MPO implementation the call uses
\texttt{apply\_dag=true} with the same wavefunction gates
$G_j=e^{-ih_j\delta t}$ used by the MPS Trotter route.  In dense-matrix tests,
the primed MPO site indices are interpreted as the row layer and the unprimed
indices as the column layer.  With this convention the MPO evolution realizes
the forward channel \eqref{eq:trotter_density_slice}.  The code tests this by
comparing ED and TN for one complete density-matrix cooling cycle, including
bath preparation and bath trace, and by checking the evolved MPO against the
corresponding pure MPS Trotter evolution before bath measurement.


After the time evolution (\ref{eq:rhot}), we perform the partial trace of the bath degrees of freedom. This can be done efficiently for matrix product operators (see Fig.~\ref{fig:MPO}(c)). Essentially, the order-four tensor of the site to be traced out is reduced to a matrix after applying the trace operation and can be merged with neighboring tensors through a contraction.

To simulate the effects of noise using the MPO method, we apply the Kraus operators corresponding to the depolarizing noise as operators on the MPO after each cooling step. The depolarizing noise is described by the channel
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{E}_\text{depl}(\rho) = (1-p_e)\rho + \frac{p_e}{3}(\sigma_x\rho \sigma_x + \sigma_y\rho \sigma_y + \sigma_z\rho\sigma_z),
	\label{eq:depolarizing}
\end{equation}
where $p_e$ represents the probability of a noise event occurring, called the noise strength. The depolarizing noise is applied to every qubit in the system.

The matrix product operator simulations were performed using the ITensor.jl~\cite{Fishman2022ITensor} library. The initial state in the MPO simulations is set to be the infinite temperature identity state. The bond dimension is truncated to a maximum value of $D \leq 100$ with a cutoff of $10^{-5}$, and the Trotter step size is set to $\delta t = 0.1$. The Hamiltonian parameters are set to $J=1$, $h_z=0.5$, and $h_x=-1.05$, corresponding to a non-integrable point in the phase diagram. The coupling strength between the system and the bath is set to $g=0.3$, and the total evolution time is $t_e=2.0$. The number of cooling steps is varied from 100 to 500.


\section{Quantum Trajectory Simulation with Matrix Product States}

As an alternative to solving the many-body dynamical equation (\ref{eq:rhokp1}) directly, the quantum trajectory method allows us to work with stochastic wavefunctions instead of density operators. This approach is advantageous because MPO simulations without purification may lead to unphysical states, even though the steady state is physical. During the evolution, the truncation might result in states with lower energies than the ground state, which is incorrect. By combining quantum trajectories with matrix product state techniques, we can efficiently simulate the cooling dynamics for large system sizes, exploiting the low-entanglement structure of the states involved in the process.


Each stochastic trajectory begins from the same initial normalized pure state $\ket{\Psi_S^1}$ and evolves according to the following procedure.
\begin{enumerate}
	\item At each step $k$, calculate the coherent time evolution according to the Schr\"{o}dinger equation with the joint Hamiltonian $H = H_S + H_B + gV$, up to $t_e$
	      \begin{equation}
		      \ket{\Psi_{SB}^{k+1}} = e^{-iHt_e} (\ket{\Psi_{S}^{k}} \otimes \ket{0}_B).
		      \label{eq:dynamics}
	      \end{equation}
	\item After the joint pure state evolution, the bath needs to be traced out. The partial trace can be expressed with an orthonormal basis of the bath $\{\ket{\alpha}\}$,
	      \begin{equation}
		      \begin{aligned}
			      \rho_S^{k+1} & = {\tr}_B [ \ket{\Psi_{SB}^{k+1}}\bra{\Psi_{SB}^{k+1}} ]                                                   \\
			                   & =  \sum_{\alpha}\Id_S \otimes {}_B\bra{\alpha}\ket{\Psi_{SB}^{k+1}} \bra{\Psi_{SB}^{k+1}}  \ket{\alpha}_B.
		      \end{aligned}
	      \end{equation}
	      To simulate the partial trace using the quantum trajectory method, we use the perfect unitary sampling method~\cite{Ferris2012Perfect} to sample the configuration of the ancillas and the trajectories. The key steps of the perfect unitary sampling method are as follows:
	      \begin{enumerate}
		      \item Compute the probability of observing each ancilla configuration by contracting the MPS tensors with the corresponding basis states.
		      \item Sample an ancilla configuration according to the computed probabilities.
		      \item Project the ancilla sites onto the sampled configuration by applying local projectors to the relevant MPS tensors and renormalizing the state.
	      \end{enumerate}
	      This method ensures that the sampled trajectories are representative of the true dynamics and avoids the need to simulate the full density matrix.
	\item The state is renormalized, and we continue with step 1 until $k = N_\text{steps}$.
\end{enumerate}

The implementation contains two time-evolution routes for MPS trajectories.
For \texttt{ContinuousEvolution}, the code uses the time-dependent variational
principle (TDVP) algorithm~\cite{Haegeman2011TimeDependent,Haegeman2016Unifying}.
For a fixed bath detuning \(\Delta\), it constructs the full interleaved
system-bath MPO \(H_{SB}(\Delta)\) and approximates
\begin{equation}
	\ket{\Psi_{SB}^{k+1}}
	=
	e^{-iH_{SB}(\Delta)t_e}
	\bigl(\ket{\Psi_S^k}\otimes\ket{\Omega_B(\Delta)}\bigr)
\end{equation}
by two-site TDVP on the MPS manifold.  In the present ITensor convention the
time parameter supplied to TDVP is \(-i t_e\), and the interval is partitioned
into
\begin{equation}
	M_{\mathrm{TDVP}}=\left\lceil \frac{t_e}{\tau}\right\rceil
\end{equation}
substeps.  The parameter \(\tau\) is therefore a target substep size, not a
Trotter gate time.  Before the two-site TDVP sweep, the implementation expands
the MPS basis by a global Krylov vector.  In formulae, if
\(\ket{\Psi_0}=\ket{\Psi_S^k}\otimes\ket{\Omega_B(\Delta)}\), then the
initial variational basis is enlarged so that it contains the Krylov space
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{K}_m(H_{SB},\ket{\Psi_0})
	=
	\operatorname{span}\{\ket{\Psi_0},H_{SB}\ket{\Psi_0},\ldots,
	H_{SB}^m\ket{\Psi_0}\}.
	\label{eq:TDVPKrylovExpansion}
\end{equation}
The code currently uses \(m=1\) by default.  This expansion is essential for
the interleaved ordering
\((S_1,B_1,S_2,B_2,\ldots)\): the nearest-neighbor Ising term
\(\sigma^z_i\sigma^z_{i+1}\) then acts on MPS sites \(2i-1\) and \(2i+1\).
From a product MPS, the unexpanded two-site TDVP tangent space does not contain
the corresponding next-nearest-neighbor first-order direction, and therefore
can incorrectly leave the state stationary for, for example, the
\(\sigma^x_S\sigma^x_B\) cooling channel.  The Krylov expansion restores this
direction and is verified against exact exponentiation at small system size.
For \texttt{TrotterEvolution}, the code instead applies the
same interleaved system-bath Trotter circuit as in the MPO calculation,
followed by stochastic measurement of the bath sites.  These two MPS routes
must be reported separately: the Trotter route is the discrete circuit used for
direct MPS/MPO channel comparisons, whereas the TDVP route is a continuous-time
variational approximation to the same Hamiltonian evolution.

To simulate the effects of noise using the MPS method, we employ a probabilistic approach. After each cooling step, we draw a random number $r$ uniformly from $[0,1]$ for each qubit. If $r < p_e$, we apply a randomly selected Pauli operator ($\sigma_x$, $\sigma_y$, or $\sigma_z$) to the corresponding qubit in the MPS. This procedure effectively simulates the action of the depolarizing noise channel described in Eq.~(\ref{eq:depolarizing}).

When the engineered channel has the ground state of $H_S$ as an attractive fixed point, and when no symmetry sector or truncation effect obstructs relaxation, the trajectories are expected to approach low-energy states. This fixed-point property is a physical condition to be checked for the chosen coupling, detuning schedule, and numerical approximation; it should not be assumed from the tensor-network representation alone.

We call $\ket{\Psi_S^{k,(i)}}$ the state of the $i$-th trajectory after $k$ cooling steps. After having collected a set of $N_\text{QT}$ samples $\{ \ket{\Psi_S^{k,(i)}} \}$, we can estimate several physical quantities.

\paragraph{Observable}
The stochastic average of the single trajectory expectation values is equal to the expectation value according to Eq.~(\ref{eq:rhokp1})
\begin{equation}
	\tr[A \rho_S^k] \approx \frac{1}{N_\text{QT}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_\text{QT}}\bra{\Psi_S^{k,(i)}}A\ket{\Psi_S^{k,(i)}}.
\end{equation}
where $A$ is an observable of interest.
We compute the energy and other observables to check whether the ground state is reached.

\paragraph{State overlaps}
The state fidelity between a given state $\ket{\Phi}$ and $\rho_S^k$ can be computed in parallel
\begin{equation}
	F = \bra{\Phi}\rho_S^k\ket{\Phi} \approx \frac{1}{N_\text{QT}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_\text{QT}} \left|\braket{\Phi|Psi_S^{k,(i)}}\right|^2.
\end{equation}
We use the state fidelity with respect to the low excited states of $H_S$ to quantify the efficiency of cooling.


A single trajectory ($N_\text{QT}=1$), together with a time average over late cooling steps, is useful as a diagnostic of a proposed protocol. It is not by itself a proof of convergence of the density-matrix channel. Quantitative steady-state estimates should compare independent trajectories, or otherwise justify the replacement of the ensemble average by a time average.


\paragraph{Purity}
The purity of the density matrix $\rho_S^k$ is not the average of the trajectory purities, and the calculation of the purity requires all the trajectories at the same time,
\begin{equation}
	\tr[(\rho_S^k)^2] \approx \frac{1}{N_\text{QT}^2} \sum_{i,i'=1}^{N_\text{QT}} \left|\braket{\Psi_S^{k,(i)}|\Psi_S^{k,(i')}}\right|^2.
\end{equation}

The matrix product state simulations were performed using the ITensor.jl~\cite{Fishman2022ITensor} library. Since an MPS quantum trajectory is a pure state simulation, its initial condition is a specified pure product state rather than the maximally mixed density matrix used in the MPO simulations. In the current simulation scripts, \texttt{init\_type="product"} denotes a uniform computational-basis product state, while \texttt{init\_type="theta"} denotes the product-state family in \eqref{eq:code-theta-map}. The identity state is therefore reserved for density-matrix simulations unless an explicit pure-state ensemble or purification is introduced.

The present large-\(N\) fixed-detuning checks should be read as bond-dimension
diagnostics, not as a final demonstration of ground-state preparation.  For
\(N=64\), \(R=1,2,5,10\), and the MPS \texttt{TrotterEvolution} route, the

\section{Bayesian Optimization for Hyperparameter Tuning}
Optimizing the performance of the cooling algorithm requires finding the optimal values for hyperparameters such as the coupling strength $g$ and the evolution time $t_e$. However, the relationship between these hyperparameters and the cooling efficiency is complex and evaluating the cooling efficiency for a given set of hyperparameters is computationally expensive. We employ Bayesian optimization, a framework for global optimization of expensive black-box functions~\cite{Frazier2018Tutorial}, to address this challenge.

Bayesian optimization constructs a probabilistic surrogate model, typically a Gaussian process~\cite{Rasmussen2006Gaussian}, of the objective function (cooling efficiency) based on observed values at different hyperparameter configurations. The surrogate model's posterior distribution guides the search for optimal hyperparameters by balancing exploration and exploitation through an acquisition function, such as Expected Improvement~\cite{Mockus1978Application} or Upper Confidence Bound~\cite{Srinivas2010Gaussian}. The acquisition function determines the next point to evaluate in the hyperparameter space, aiming to maximize the expected improvement over the current best solution while considering the uncertainty in the surrogate model's predictions.

At each iteration, the hyperparameter configuration that maximizes the acquisition function is selected, and the objective function is evaluated at that point. The new observation is then used to update the surrogate model, and the process is repeated until a predefined budget (e.g., number of iterations or time limit) is exhausted. Bayesian optimization is particularly suitable when the objective function is expensive to evaluate, as it aims to minimize the number of function evaluations required to find the global optimum.

We use the BOHB (Bayesian Optimization and Hyperband) algorithm~\cite{Falkner2018BOHB}, which combines Bayesian optimization with Hyperband~\cite{Li2017Hyperband}, a bandit-based strategy for hyperparameter optimization. The BOHB algorithm is implemented using the Hyperopt.jl library~\cite{Hyperopt.jl}. We define the objective function as the average energy density of the last $k=100$ steps of the MPS quantum trajectory simulation. The search space for the coupling strength $g$ is set to the range $[0.1, 0.5]$, and the search space for the evolution time $t_e$ is set to the range $[1.0, 3.0]$. We run the optimization for 20 iterations, with each cooling simulation running for $200$ steps to evaluate the objective function. The best hyperparameter configuration found by the Bayesian optimization procedure is then used to run the final cooling simulations with $800$ steps and evaluate the optimized algorithm's performance. The Bayesian optimization is performed using the Hyperopt.jl library~\cite{Hyperopt.jl} for the MPS trajectory simulation.

\subsection{Model}
We test the performance of our algorithm on a one-dimensional quantum Ising model, with the Hamiltonian given by (open boundary condition)
\begin{equation}
	H_{\text{Ising}} = J\sum_{i=1}^{N-1}\sigma_{i}^z\sigma_{i+1}^z + h_z\sum_{i=1}^{N} \sigma_{i}^z + h_x\sum_{i=1}^{N} \sigma_{i}^x.
	\label{eq:HIsing}
\end{equation}
where $J$ is the coupling strength between neighboring spins, $h_z$ and $h_x$ are the transverse and longitudinal magnetic fields, respectively, and $\sigma_i^{\alpha}$ ($\alpha = x,y,z$) are the Pauli operators acting on the $i$-th spin.
This ZZXZ Hamiltonian is in general non-integrable, except in the limits $h_z = 0$ (classical) and $h_x = 0$ (transverse field Ising model). We focus on a non-integrable point $J = 1,h_z = 0.5,h_x = -1.05$, the same as used in Ref.~\citep{Lu2021Algorithms}.

\subsection{Initial states}
As in Ref.~\citep{Lu2021Algorithms}, we consider real translationally invariant product states. It is useful to distinguish the physical product-state angle from the parameter used in the simulation scripts. We write the physical angle as $\alpha$ and define
\begin{equation}
	\ket{\Psi(\alpha)} = \ket{p(\alpha)}^{\otimes N},
	\label{eq:PS}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
	\ket{p(\alpha)} = \cos\alpha\,\ket{0} + \sin\alpha\,\ket{1}.
\end{equation}
In the thermodynamic limit, these states have energy density
\begin{equation}
	\frac{E(\alpha)}{N}
	= J\cos^2(2\alpha) + h_z \cos(2\alpha) + h_x \sin(2\alpha),
	\label{eq:product-state-energy-alpha}
\end{equation}
ranging over most of the energy band (see Fig.~\ref{fig:ELNI}).

The code path \texttt{init\_type="theta"} uses a dimensionless input parameter, denoted here by $\vartheta$, rather than the physical angle $\alpha$ itself. The convention is
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		\alpha(\vartheta)
		&= \frac{\pi}{2}\left(\vartheta + \frac{1}{2}\right),\\
		\ket{p(\vartheta)}
		&= \cos\alpha(\vartheta)\ket{0}
		+ \sin\alpha(\vartheta)\ket{1}.
	\end{aligned}
	\label{eq:code-theta-map}
\end{equation}
Thus $\vartheta=-1/2,0,1/2$ give respectively $\ket{0}$, $\ket{+}$, and $\ket{1}$. Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		\frac{E(\vartheta)}{N}
		&= J\cos^2\!\left[\pi\left(\vartheta+\frac{1}{2}\right)\right]\\
		&\quad + h_z\cos\!\left[\pi\left(\vartheta+\frac{1}{2}\right)\right]\\
		&\quad + h_x\sin\!\left[\pi\left(\vartheta+\frac{1}{2}\right)\right].
	\end{aligned}
	\label{eq:product-state-energy-code-theta}
\end{equation}

For the initial states, we choose three physical angles in the lower part of the energy band, namely $\alpha_1 = \pi/4$, for which $E_1/N = h_x = -1.05$, $\alpha_2 = \pi/3$, for which $E_2/N = -0.909$, and $\alpha_3 = \pi/6$, with $E_3/N = -0.409$. In the code convention these correspond to $\vartheta_1=0$, $\vartheta_2=1/6$, and $\vartheta_3=-1/6$. These product states already have rather low energies, and to gain quantum advantage, the quantum cooling algorithm must obtain lower energy in the presence of noise. In the following numerical experiments, we will test the performance of the cooling algorithm starting from these three translationally invariant product states.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Optimization Results}

To optimize the performance of the cooling algorithm, we employ Bayesian optimization to find the optimal values for the coupling strength $g$ and the evolution time $t_e$. The objective function is defined as the average energy density of the last $k=100$ steps of the cooling simulation, and the search space for $g$ and $t_e$ is set to $[0.1, 0.5]$ and $[1.0, 3.0]$, respectively. The optimization is performed using the BOHB algorithm, with 20 trials and each cooling simulation running for 200 steps to evaluate the objective function.

We observe that the optimal coupling strength $g^*$ decreases with increasing system size and noise rate, while the optimal evolution time $t_e^*$ increases. This suggests that for larger systems and stronger noise, a weaker coupling and longer evolution time are required to achieve optimal cooling performance.

To evaluate the performance of the optimized cooling algorithm, we run the cooling simulations using the optimal hyperparameter values for different system sizes and noise rates. Fig.~\ref{fig:OptimizedCooling} shows the late-time energy density and ground state overlap obtained from the optimized simulations. Within the historical truncation settings, the optimized parameters give lower energy densities and higher ground state overlaps than the unoptimized parameters. The same parameters should be re-evaluated with the current fixed-detuning, bond-dimension, and trajectory-convergence diagnostics before being used as evidence for scalable cooling.
